Buddhism

Who Is The Buddha?

Siddhārtha Gautama , known as the Buddha , was a spiritual seeker who lived in India in the 6th century BC . He is regarded as the founder of the Buddhist religion and is praised by most Buddhist schools as a savior who rediscovered an ancient path to destroy attachment and craving in order to be free from suffering and the cycle of rebirth . He taught for an estimated 45 to 49 years and built a massive following of monastic and lay (non-monastic) practitioners. His teachings are based on his insight into the emergence of suffering/dissatisfaction and the ending of this suffering/dissatisfaction which can be reached by attaining a heightened mental state called Nirvana , commonly known as reaching enlightenment or Buddhahood . Buddhists believe this state of no more suffering is available to be attained this lifetime by anyone who gathers the necessary wisdom, compassion, concertation, and merit of living an extremely ethical or holy life.

The Buddha was born into a royal family in the Shakya clan where his father tried to hide all signs of suffering from him within the palace he grew up in. No old person, sick person, poor person, or any mention of human suffering was seen or spoke of to the then prince who became the Buddha. All suffering was intentionally coveted from him. This was until the Buddha first observed the suffering of the world while on chariot rides outside the palace where he saw poor people who were sick, hungry, and dying. This led to the Buddha eventually renouncing his royal life leaving his wife, infant child, and royalty behind for his quest to find a solution to end suffering and seek spiritual advancement. After several straight years of meditation , being a beggar, and extreme self-discipline he awakened (attained enlightenment) to understand the workings which keep people to experience suffering and the products thereof. He famously reached Nirvana sitting in meditation under a Bodhi tree .

The Buddha then traveled throughout apart of India called the Indo-Gangetic Plain teaching and building a religious community. He taught mind training that included moral and ethical practice, self-restraint, and meditative practice. The Buddha ultimately taught a path for people to follow which leads to attaining a mind conditioned to no longer experience suffering or dissatisfaction. The Buddha’s path teaches a middle way between sensual indulgence and the severe and extreme self-discipline found in the Indian spiritual seeker movement such as extreme fasting or years of solitude.

The Buddha's teachings were collected and later transcribed by the Buddhist community. The teachings originally (and currently) were passed down through an oral tradition . This led to later generations composing additional texts, such as systematic comprehensive paths to enlightenment, biographies of the Buddha and collections of stories about the Buddha's past lives.


Buddhism in Tibet

Buddhism was brought to Tibet from India by an Indian Buddhist Abbot called Shantarakshita and the legendary “Lotus-Born Master” Guru Padmasambhava at around the 6th to 9th century AD. Buddhism was then banned during the 9th and 10th century only to resurface around the 11th century. Since the 11th century Buddhism has become rife within Tibet. Tibetan Buddhism now consists of 7 main lineages of Buddhism of which almost the entire country practices under and has been for centuries.

Guru Padmasambhava, also known as Guru Rinpoché

Tibetan Buddhism has distinct cultural and religious qualities compared to other Buddhist practices. This is due to Tibet’s own unique culture and the ideologies and philosophies of the Buddhist masters they chose to follow over the centuries. Tibetan Buddhists follow a sect of Buddhism called Mahāyāna which is a term for a broad group of Buddhist traditions(lineages), texts, philosophies, and practices.

Mahāyāna accepts the main scriptures and teachings of early Buddhism, but also adds various new doctrines and texts such which put emphasis on areas of the Buddhist path which make use of numerous additional traditional methods considered to be faster and more powerful at achieving Buddhahood . Mahāyāna translates to the greater vehicle , the vehicle being the figurative mode of transport to enlightenment.

Mahāyāna points the practitioner to strive to become a fully awakened enlightened being (Buddha) for the benefit of all sentient beings whereas non-Mahāyāna ( Hinyāna or lesser vehicle ) traditions focus on enlightenment exclusively for oneself . Mahāyāna Buddhism generally sees the goal of becoming enlightened as being available to all. Mahāyāna also includes numerous Buddhas and other figures that are not found in other sectors of Buddhism. In the course of its history, Mahāyāna Buddhism spread throughout most parts of Asia. Mahāyāna Buddhism remains influential today in most of Asia.

The Mahāyāna tradition is the largest major tradition of Buddhism existing today and compromises of 53% of all Buddhists . There are 535 million people who practice Buddhism as a whole. The most well-known living figure among Mahayana Buddhists is His Holiness the Dalai Lama who is Tibet's former political and spiritual leader .
