Tibet is a country that has been described as a land “stuck in time”, full of myth and mystery. Tibet’s unchanging essence is partially due to Tibet shutting its doors completely to the outside world in the 1850s for over 100 years due to political reasons. This led to Tibet conserving its unique unchanging traditional ways . Even today people can be seen fully dressed in their traditional ethnic ordainments, just as it would have been centuries ago, roaming around clay temples and buildings dating back as far as 997 AD. Tibet is covered with ancient Buddhist monasteries and temples which have been a hub for Buddhist activity for centuries. Learn more about Buddhism

One may wonder how is it possible for a country to just simply shut its doors to the outside world? The answer is that Tibet is situated 5000 meters above sea level and is surrounded by Himalayan Mountain peaks lying between an average of 6000 and 7500 meters tall. Mount Everest , the highest point on earth is 8800 meters tall and lies on Tibet’s border. Tibet is described as the “roof of the world” and is undoubtedly one of the harshest inhabited climates on earth due to its low oxygen levels and subsequently barren land. Despite Tibet’s arid climate, there’s wonderful hikes and tours up the Himalayas, around religious sites and even the world’s highest saltwater lake. See hiking in Tibet

Since 1959, Tibet has been under strict control under the government of People's Republic of China . For over 1500 years Tibet has had complicated relationships with China where the balance of power has shifted back and forth. China currently occupies Tibet . Many Tibetans now live all over the world in exile while foreign policy tries to help regain a free and independent Tibet . Read more China and free Tibet campaign
